Random variables

Randomness

  • A random process is a definite outcome that cannot be predicted with certainty

    • Coin flip

    • Student test scores (for example, your EC2203 mark)

    • Waiting time in the College shop

    • The FTSE100 index

    • Salary in your first job

  • We use random variables to analyze situations involving randomness

Outcomes, sample space, and events

  • Mutually exclusive potential results of a random process are called outcomes

  • The set of all possible outcomes is called the sample space, and is typically denoted \(\Omega\)

  • An event is a subset of the sample space \(\Omega\)

Probability

  • Let \(P\) be probability measure. \(P(E)\) is the probability that event \(E\) is realized

  • To be a probability measure, \(P\) must satisfy the three Axioms of Probability. The axioms implies that \(P\) has the intuitive properties that

    \[0 \leq P(E) \leq 1; \quad P(\Omega) = 1\]

  • \(P(E)\) is the proportion of times \(E\) is realized in repeated, independent trials of the random process.

Random variables

  • Numerical summary of a random outcome

  • A random variable \(X\) assigns a real number to every element in a sample space

    \[X: \Omega \mapsto \mathbb{R}\]

Distributions

Distributions

  • A random variable has a distribution that represents the likelihood that any of the possible values would occur

  • Distinguish b/w discrete and continuous random variables

    • Discrete: take a countable number of values

    • Continuous: take values on a continuum

The PMF and CDF of a discrete random variable

  • The probability mass function (PMF) (or probability distribution) of a discrete random variable \(X\) is

    \[f_X(x) = P(X = x); \quad \sum_x f_X(x) = 1\]

  • The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a discrete random variable \(X\) is is

    \[F_X(x) = P(X \leq x)\]

The CDF of a continuous random variable

  • The CDF of a continuous random variable \(X\) is defined exactly as for discrete random variables:

    \[F_X(x) = P(X \leq x)\]

The probability density function

  • The PMF is inappropriate for continuous random variables; instead, introduce the probability density function (PDF)

  • The area under the PDF \(f_X(X)\) b/w two points is equal to the probability that \(X\) fall b/w those two points

    \[\int_a^b f_X(x) dx = F_X(b) - F_X(a)\]

  • The PDF is the derivative of the CDF, and integrates to 1

    \[f_X(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F_X(x); \quad \int f_X(x) dx = 1\]

Expectations

The expected value of a random variable

  • The expected value of \(Y\), denoted \(\textrm{E}(Y)\), is the average value of \(Y\) over many repeated, independent occurrences

    \[\textrm{E}(Y) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \sum_y y f_Y(y) & \text{if } Y \text{ discrete} \\[0.5ex] \int y f_Y(y) dy & \text{if } Y \text{ continuous} \end{array} \right.\]

  • \(\textrm{E}(Y)\) is the mean of \(Y\), and is sometimes denoted \(\mu_Y\)

  • The mean measures the location of a distribution

Variance of a random variable

  • The variance of \(Y\), denoted \(\textrm{var}(Y)\), is the expected value of the squared deviation of \(Y\) from its mean:

    \[\textrm{var}(Y) = \textrm{E}[(Y-\mu_Y)^2]\]

  • The variance is sometimes denoted \(\sigma^2_Y\)

  • The variance measures the spread of a distribution

The standard deviation of a random variable

  • The standard deviation is the square root of the variance, and is denoted \(\textrm{sd}(Y)\) or \(\sigma_Y\)

    \[\sigma_Y = \sqrt{\sigma^2_Y}\]

  • The standard deviation is measured in units of \(Y\)

A standardized random variable

  • A random variable \(Y\) can be transformed into a random variable with mean 0 and variance 1

    • subtract the mean of \(Y\), \(\mu_Y\),

    • divide by the standard deviation of \(Y\), \(\sigma_Y\),

  • This process is called standardization; the standardized random variable computed from \(Y\) is

    \[\frac{Y - \mu_Y}{\sigma_Y}\]

  • Standardized random variables are unit-free

Summary

Summary

  • A random variable assigns a number to a random outcome

  • The probabilities with which a random variable takes on different values are summarized by the PMF/PDF (for discrete/continuous random variables) and the CDF

  • The expected value of a random variable is the probability or density weighted average of the random variable

  • The mean of a random variable measures the location of its distribution.

  • The standard deviation of a random variable measures the spread of its distribution.
